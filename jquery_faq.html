10 Facts about National Parks

Yellowstone National Park
Yellowstone National Park (in Wyoming, Idaho, and Montana) is the oldest U.S. national park, founded in 1872.
Yosemite National Park
Three of the 10 highest waterfalls in the world are located in Yosemite National Park in California.
Death Valley National Park
Death Valley National Park is the largest national park in the lower 48 U.S. states, stretching 5,300 square miles or 3.4 million acres.
Arches National Park
Arches National Park is famous for having the largest concentration of stone arches in the world.
Rocky Mountain National Park
Trail Ridge Road in Rocky Mountain National Park, which connects Grand Lake and Estes Park, is the highest continuously paved road in the U.S., and it includes breathtaking views and vistas along its entire 48 miles.
Crater Lake National Park
At 1,932 feet deep, Crater Lake National Park in Oregon is the deepest lake in the U.S.
Denali National Park
The highest point in North America is Mount McKinley (aka Denali) standing at 20,320ft (6193.5m). It is in Denali National Park and Preserve, Alaska.
Mammoth Cave National Park
The longest cave system in the world is at Mammoth Cave National Park, Kentucky. Currently, there is more than 3454 miles of cave mapped, with more to come. The largest “room” in the portion of the cave system that has been discovered is two acres in size.
Arctic National Park & Kobuk Valley National Park
Two national parks are located north of the Arctic Circle: the Gates of the Arctic National Park and the Kobuk Valley National Park, both in Alaska.

Yellowstone National Park (in Wyoming, Idaho, and Montana) is the oldest U.S. national park, founded in 1872.

Hot Springs National Park

  • Native Americans referred to the hot springs at Hot Springs National Park as 'Valley of the Vapors'. They have been using the springs for at least 8000 years. The springs are included in many Native American legends.
  • In the 1870s the waters of Hot Springs were being prescribed by doctors for various ailments. Patients were advised to spend a specific amount of time in the tub baths and steam baths depending on the illness.
  • Hot Springs National Park is one of the few national parks that make up a part of a city rather than being located miles from developed infrastructure.
  • The thermal waters at Hot Springs National Park can be enjoyed by its visitors. The water in the thermal pools is 143 degrees Fahrenheit.

Hawai'i Volcanoes National Park

  • Hawaii Volcanoes National Park has 7 ecological zones. These include alpine, subalpine, upland forest, rain forest, mid-elevation woodland, lowland, and seacoast.
  • There are 59 endangered species living in Hawaii Volcanoes National Park. These include, among others, the Hawaiian hoary bat, Hawaiian goose, Hawaiian petrel, and hawksbill turtle.
  • Plant diversity at the park is so diverse there are 1000 different species found in its borders. Of these 1000 plant species, 90% are found only in the park's region.
  • The active volcanoes in Hawaii Volcanoes National Park continue to resurface the land because the lava continues to flow. This results in a constantly changing environment.

Badlands National Park

  • Fossils of prehistoric creatures dating back 30 million years have been found in the Badlands National Park region including rhinoceroses, and prehistoric camels.
  • President Franklin D. Roosevelt created Badlands National Monument by signing a proclamation.
  • French Canadians referred to the area as 'bad lands to travel through.'
  • The name 'badlands' is believed to originate from the Lakota people.
